## Overview

This is an exploration of what it might look like to redesign and illustrate a new English translation of Homer's *Odyssey* — the kind of object that lives on a coffee table or a bookshelf and earns its weight. The brief is partly real, partly imagined: a publisher who wants something more bookish than airport, more confident than a school edition, and a designer who wants to spend a long time looking at sea, fire, and weaving.

The work runs in three registers — a typographic system for the text, a sequence of full-bleed illustrations for the principal episodes, and a small set of marginalia and chapter ornaments. What follows is the visual direction and the choices that pulled it together.

## Reading the source

Before any visuals, a long pass through the text. I read three translations side by side — Lattimore for fidelity, Fagles for cadence, Wilson for clarity — and underlined every concrete object that recurs: the wine-dark sea, the rosy-fingered dawn, the well-built ship, the gleaming threshold, the loom. The poem is more architectural than I'd remembered. Spaces matter as much as people. The cave of the Cyclops is a different room than the hall of the Phaeacians; the threshold of Odysseus's own house, when he finally crosses it, has been waiting twenty years.

The visual direction starts there: this is a book about returning, and returning means returning *to* a place. The illustrations should let you stand inside each room.

## Visual direction

Three principles, fixed before any drawings:

- **Heavy line, soft fill.** Confident outline weight — closer to a printer's woodcut than to a graphic novel — with watercolor or gradient fills that don't quite stay inside the line. The reader's eye should catch the line first and the color second.
- **One palette per episode.** Each major episode gets a three-or-four-color palette pulled from the recurring imagery in its own text. Polyphemus is fire-orange and bone-white. Circe is wine-red and laurel-green. The Sirens are sea-grey and rust. Across the book the palettes travel from warm to cool to warm again, mirroring Odysseus's own arc.
- **Type that doesn't argue with the illustrations.** A quiet, slightly old-fashioned serif for body. Display moments get a single all-caps face cut by hand for this project — used sparingly, only at chapter openings.

## The wandering

The middle of the *Odyssey* is structured as a series of stories Odysseus tells the Phaeacians — a frame, then nine inset episodes. Each gets a full-bleed illustration as its opening spread.

### The Cyclops

Polyphemus's cave is the book's first violence. The illustration leans into scale: the giant doesn't fit on the page, only one of his eyes and one hand. The Greeks are smaller than the bones of the giant's earlier meals. Color: hot, dim, very few light sources.

### Circe

Circe's hall is the book's first seduction. The illustration is composed like a Renaissance interior — deep perspective, framed view through an arch, the witch at her loom in the middle distance. The men-turned-pigs are at the edges. The reader should understand the room before they understand what's happened in it.

### The Sirens

The Sirens get the most restraint of any episode — almost monochrome, almost off-page. The composition is a horizon: ship low, Sirens high, song rendered as a band of color in the sky. The reader should feel the pull, not be told about it.

### Scylla and Charybdis

Two pages, one image — split vertically. Scylla on the verso, Charybdis on the recto, the ship pinned between them in the gutter. The reader physically turns the page through the strait.

### Calypso

A long, quiet illustration. Seven years on the same island compressed into a single image. Composition: Odysseus seated on a shore, the sea flat and pale, Calypso standing behind him at a distance, both looking at different things. This is the only spread that's intentionally boring — to give the reader the weight of the time.

### The Phaeacians

The ferrying-home episode. Lighter palette, brighter line, almost a relief after the wandering chapters. This is where the reader gets to breathe.

## Characters

A small set of character studies sits between the major episodes — half-page portraits, more diagrammatic than narrative.

- **Odysseus** appears in three states: the captain, the beggar, the bowman. Same line, very different color — sun-bronzed, dust-grey, gold-and-blood.
- **Athena** is rendered as light, never as a body. She arrives as an angle of brightness on a wall, on a sail, on a face.
- **Penelope** is at the loom in every appearance. The loom *is* her portrait. Twenty years of unweaving live in the tension of the threads.
- **Telemachus** changes the most. The reader sees him grow over the course of the book — first sketch is a boy, last sketch is a young man with a sword.

## Color and atmosphere

A book of this length needs a color score, not just a palette. I plotted every spread on a long horizontal strip, sketched in to its dominant hue, and read it like a film print. The arc lands at:

- Books I–IV: cool greys and dusty blues — Telemachus searching.
- Books V–XII: a long warm middle — fire, wine, blood, sun on water.
- Books XIII–XIX: cool again — disguise, return, the cold edge of Ithaca.
- Books XX–XXIV: warm but different — gold rather than fire, recognition rather than survival.

The reader doesn't read this consciously, but they feel it. The book runs hotter and colder by chapter. By the end the warmth is earned.

## Typography

- **Body face:** a custom-feeling old-style serif — Caslon-adjacent, slightly low contrast, with a generous x-height so it reads at the long line measure the page calls for. Set 11/16 across a 28-pica column.
- **Display face:** a single all-caps face cut for this book. Stone-cut feeling, slightly compressed, used only at the opening of each Book and for the names of gods. Hand-spaced.
- **Marginalia:** the body serif at small size, italic, used for the briefest of running glosses ("the wine-dark sea," "rosy-fingered dawn") so the reader's eye has somewhere to rest.

## The homecoming

The last quarter of the book is its own visual register. The illustrations stop being landscapes and start being interiors and faces. The bow scene is composed almost entirely of hands — Odysseus's, the suitors', Penelope's testing hand on the bedpost. The recognition between Odysseus and Penelope is two faces close together with a single shared pool of warm light.

The very last spread is empty. A single line, low, on a cool field — *the bed they had made together, rooted in the living olive tree*. Nothing else.

## Pulling it together

The final system is small: one body face, one display face, eleven episode palettes, a consistent illustration grammar. The book reads as one object across 530 pages.

A short summary of what's loadbearing:

- **Heavy line, soft fill** keeps every spread feeling like part of the same book.
- **One palette per episode** lets each story feel distinct without breaking the whole.
- **Display face used sparingly** keeps it earning its weight.
- **Cool–warm–cool–warm pacing** gives the long middle a structure the eye can follow.

## What I took from it

- A long book is a long composition, not a stack of short ones. Plotting color and pacing across the whole thing matters as much as getting any single spread right.
- Restraint scales. The Sirens spread is the most restrained illustration in the book and arguably the strongest — because every other spread leaned into it.
- Spaces are characters. The cave, the hall, the loom-room, the bedchamber — these aren't backgrounds, they're the things the poem is actually about.
- A type choice for a book is a sound decision more than a visual one. The reader hears the body face for hours. It needs to have a voice that doesn't tire.

A book is the long form. It rewards the long look.
