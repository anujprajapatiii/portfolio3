## Overview

Kepler-186f is 582 light-years away, in the constellation Cygnus, orbiting a red dwarf cooler and dimmer than our Sun. It was the first planet roughly the size of Earth ever found in the habitable zone of another star — close enough to its sun for liquid water, small enough to plausibly be rocky.

This is a short visual field guide to that planet: what we know, what we don't, and how a place we will never visit can still be carefully described.

## What we know

The planet is about 1.17 times the radius of Earth. It completes an orbit every 130 days. Its star is roughly half the size of the Sun and gives off a third of the energy. From Kepler-186f's surface at noon, the light would feel like an hour before sunset on Earth — dimmer, redder, slower.

That is, more or less, the whole list.

## What we don't

We don't know its mass. We don't know whether it has an atmosphere, or water, or weather, or seasons. We don't know whether it is tidally locked — one face permanently lit, one in eternal dusk — or whether it turns. We have a name, an orbit, and a size, and almost everything else is a question.

## Direction

The visual brief was restraint. A planet we cannot photograph should not be illustrated as if we had photographed it. The guide is built from three things only:

- A muted palette pulled from what we _do_ know — the red of an M-dwarf star, the grey of unknown rock, the deep of a sky we have not seen.
- Diagrams over scenes. Orbits, scales, and silhouettes carry more than imagined landscapes.
- One short paragraph per spread. The text earns the space.

## What I took from it

A field guide to something you cannot see is mostly a guide to your own honesty. The interesting work isn't the speculation — it's drawing the line, clearly, between what is measured and what is imagined.

The planet is real. The afternoon light is a guess.
